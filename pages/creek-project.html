The Creek Project

Conejo Valley Botanic Garden · Jan 2019 – Mar 2022

I began the Creek Project, with a mission of assisting the Conejo Valley Botanic Garden in their efforts to maintain the Garden's natural beauty with a focus on the capture of invasive crayfish and the mitigation of their growth.

01 — Crayfish Mitigation

Crayfish are a non-native species in California and constantly wreak havoc on ecosystems due to the lack of local predators. To combat this in my own community, I led a team of about 10 volunteers weekly in capturing crayfish and measuring the number of crayfish caught and their length. We did this consistently for three years, using the data from the first year to create a baseline of crayfish activity ​in every month, and using the data from later years to measure the effects of our solutions, such as watercress plantings and algae removal.

After our weekly trappings, I would import all our data into Excel and find the average number of crayfish and size at each location, and create a graph of the distribution for that day. At the end of the year, this data was summed up into one sheet and into ArcGIS, with charts representing the crayfish fluctuations in each location and the crayfish fluctuations overall. From this data, I found that the crayfish were the least active from late October to early April, so the team and I focused our trapping efforts on the summer months and used the winter months to analyze our data and research solutions.

Through our efforts, we were able to decrease the crayfish population by about 10 percent.

02 — Soil and Water Analysis

During our weekly meetings, the team and I would take measurements on the water quality through its dissolved oxygen content, pH, and total dissolved solids. Similar to our crayfish measurements, we utilized these measurements to create a yearlong baseline and measure the effects of our solutions.

On top of that, these measurements were used to understand how exactly crayfish affected the surrounding area. In areas of high crayfish activity, it was found that there were decreased dissolved oxygen levels and increased total dissolved solids, creating an environment that was inhospitable for many of the species that previously inhabited the area. Therefore, I focused the team's efforts not only on crayfish removal, but also on the mitigation of their effects on the environment.

​By the end of my time with the project, we had increased the dissolved oxygen levels by up to 5 mg/L, bringing the levels from an inhospitable 4 to 6 mg/L to about 7 or 8 mg/L, resulting in a massive growth of the amphibian community at the creek.

03 — Solutions

To combat crayfish growth and minimize their negative effects on the creek, the team and I tested a few different solutions, but our most impactful solution by far was the implementation of watercress. During one of our research sessions, when looking into solutions that had been implemented in other creeks, we found watercress.

Watercress addressed many of our issues: it was a native species that naturally improved water quality and provided spaces for tadpoles to hide from crayfish. It also was easy to source and easy to grow.

In all of this, I led the team in deciding where to plant the watercress and subsequently, where to focus our trapping efforts. I chose areas that were shallow for our plantings, because they would not only encourage watercress growth, but also provide a natural barrier to crayfish movement up and downstream. I constantly walked the length of the creek to survey for the best locations, becoming no stranger to slips and falls into the creek.

So, the team and I then began strategically planting watercress, effectively ensuring that the crayfish had nowhere to go other than in our traps. Through this, the setting of our traps became much more efficient, leading to our 10% decrease in crayfish population and 30% increase in dissolved oxygen.

04 — Exposure Efforts

Through months of careful planning, the Earth Day Event at the Conejo Valley Botanic Garden hosted over 200 attendees, educating attendees while providing them the tools to spread change in their communities.

In the Bobbing for Crayfish activity, volunteers led tours around the creek, ultimately ending at a crayfish-dense area where attendees had the chance to catch their own crayfish by sticking a pole with a piece of gum at the end of it into the water. This game relied on luck and reflexes and made the capture of crayfish into an activity that people of all ages could enjoy partaking in, shining a positive light on our removal efforts.​

In another activity, Papermaking, we used shredded paper and a mesh net to allow people to make their own sheets of paper, which they could then decorate with invasive ivy leaves and flowers from the Botanic Garden. Through this, we showed attendees invasive plants that they may encounter in their own backyards and fun ways that they can use these plants.

Then, as people were leaving, we would hand them an easy to raise native plant. Regardless of location, the plant was likely to survive and improve its local ecosystem. simplified the need for external circuitry while still providing a consistent source.
