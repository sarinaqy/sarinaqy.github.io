Vehicle Mold Design and Enclosures Lead

UC Berkeley Solar Vehicle Racing Team · Sept 2022 – Present

As a member of CalSol, I lead the enclosures team, working on the multidisciplinary integration of electronics and solar components into the vehicle. Previously, I was on the composites team, where I worked on aerodynamics validation and composites testing for our tenth-generation vehicle, Excalibur.

01 — Draft Angle Layup Testing

As Vehicle Mold Design Lead, I focused on developing and validating mold geometries for the composite vehicle body, with particular attention to draft angles and manufacturability. To evaluate how different draft angles would affect part release and layup quality, I designed and fabricated a series of test molds using 3D printing. These prototypes allowed for rapid iteration and physical testing of demolding behavior, surface finish, and composite layup consistency.

After identifying promising geometries, I transitioned to producing molds using the same materials and processes intended for the final vehicle layup, allowing us to validate manufacturing methods under realistic conditions. Through repeated testing and iteration, including direct evaluation of draft angles during demolding, we refined the mold design and layup workflow to ensure a smooth and reliable manufacturing process when producing the full vehicle body.

02 — Enclosures Manufacturing

When manufacturing the enclosures, the primary considerations were the material properties required to protect onboard electronics while minimizing overall system weight. The enclosures needed to withstand outdoor operating conditions, including exposure to rain and prolonged UV radiation, while maintaining structural integrity and meeting strict mass constraints.

Two primary material options were evaluated: ABS and polypropylene (PP). ABS offered greater environmental protection and lower cost, but at the expense of increased weight. After evaluating these tradeoffs, I implemented a hybrid material strategy. ABS was used for enclosures housing more sensitive electrical components requiring greater environmental protection, while PP was used for the remaining systems where weight savings were prioritized. This approach preserved necessary environmental protection while reducing enclosure mass by 35% compared to previous designs.

03 — Enclosures Integration

During vehicle integration, the primary considerations for enclosure placement were minimizing wiring complexity while ensuring easy access for maintenance and removal. Determining appropriate locations required extensive cross-team coordination, as each subsystem team preferred to place their enclosures near their own components. However, physical constraints within the vehicle meant that ideal placements often conflicted with other systems. As the vehicle layout continued to evolve throughout integration, I iterated on enclosure placement multiple times, balancing wiring efficiency, accessibility, and spatial constraints to arrive at locations that optimized overall system integration.

The final placement introduced additional mounting challenges, as several enclosures required upside-down installation. To ensure reliable adhesion under these conditions, I conducted extensive testing across ten different epoxies. Each adhesive was evaluated for its ability to bond to the materials present in the system, including carbon fiber, polyolefin, ABS, and polypropylene. Initial testing revealed inconsistent curing due to low ambient temperatures, which negatively affected bond strength. After accounting for temperature effects in subsequent trials, ET5428 epoxy was selected for its strong adhesion across all tested materials as well as its cost effectiveness. This solution provided a reliable mounting method capable of supporting the enclosures under the vehicle’s operational conditions.

04 — Aerodynamic Analysis

Due to the limitations of being a student-led team, we did not have access to a wind tunnel large enough to test the full-scale vehicle. To validate the aerodynamic simulations, I developed a scaled testing approach using the UC Berkeley Hesse Wind Tunnel. As an initial validation step, I tested a 3D-printed sphere and compared the recorded drag data against both analytical predictions and simulated results for spheres at the tested size as well as at a scale representative of the vehicle. This allowed me to verify that the experimental setup and simulation pipeline produced consistent aerodynamic behavior.

After validating the testing methodology, I conducted wind tunnel testing on a scaled model of the vehicle. The measured aerodynamic data from the model was then compared with the simulated results for the full-scale car to evaluate the accuracy of the simulation predictions. This process provided confidence that the aerodynamic simulations closely represented real-world behavior, allowing the team to make informed design decisions and approach competition with a stronger understanding of how the vehicle would perform.

05 — Shell Repairs

In the year prior to my involvement with CalSol, the team built the shell of the car, but due to issues in the baking process, the shell was not structurally sound. In sharp corners, the aluminum hex core had peeled away from the outer layers of carbon fiber. Our attempts to attach new layers of carbon fiber to the aluminum hex core failed due to issues with the wax used in the original carbon fiber, causing the new layers of carbon fiber to not stick.

I, along with my teammates, remedied this situation by testing for combinations of chemicals that would remove the wax. Many chemicals didn't work well on the wax by itself, but by creating a process with many different chemicals, I was able to remove enough of the wax to allow new carbon fiber layers to stick to the old carbon fiber, but not remove too much of the wax, resulting in overexposed carbon fiber.​
